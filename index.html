What it is

One wall becomes a full
shooting range.

SNYPE HOCKEY is a projection trainer. Point a projector at a wall or a shooting tarp, aim a camera at the play surface, and the room turns into a rink complete with a goalie that plays your angle.

  • Shoot from anywhere in the arc — the goalie repositions to cut the angle.
  • Green net flash on a goal, orange on a post, a HAT-TRICK banner on three in a row.
  • Runs on one Windows machine — the game, rendering and scoring all live in Unity.
  • Fully self-contained: no wearables, no pucks to charge, nothing to strap on.

How it works

From slapshot to scoreboard in milliseconds

Four stages run in a tight loop, every single shot.

  1. 01

    The camera watches

    One angled camera streams the projected play surface at 720p, keeping only the freshest frame for the lowest possible lag.

  2. 02

    AI finds the puck

    A trained detector catches the puck even as a fast, blurred streak in flight — then pinpoints the exact moment and point of impact.

  3. 03

    Unity scores it

    The impact maps to the goal and the same rules that ran the original trainer decide GOAL, SAVE, POST or MISS — with the shot's speed.

  4. 04

    You feel it land

    The net flashes, the score ticks, XP and your SNYPE rank update. Line up the next puck and go again.

The tech

Engineered around the shot

The hard part isn't drawing a net — it's knowing precisely where and when a fast puck hit it.

<100 mstarget latency from impact to on-screen feedback
~10 msAI detection per frame on a modern GPU
720pfreshest-frame capture, tuned so the camera never becomes the bottleneck
4verdicts — GOAL / SAVE / POST / MISS — decided on every shot

Vision that sees the streak

A slow puck is an easy dark disc; a slapshot in flight is a bright, blurred motion streak. A trained detector catches both, so the sensor tracks the whole flight — not just the puck once it has already slowed.

Direction-vector impact

Impact is detected where the puck's travel direction turns — the corner in its path — measured on displacement so jittery camera timing can't distort it. That reports the true contact point, even on shots that deflect and keep moving.

Sensor and game, cleanly split

The camera pipeline is just the sensor: it reports where and how hard the puck hit, and when. Unity owns all game logic, rendering, scoring and UI — one clean contract between them.

Technology serves the game — never the other way around.
